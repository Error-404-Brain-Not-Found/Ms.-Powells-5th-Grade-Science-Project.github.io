How Do We Know That Matter Particles Are There?

Matter is everything! However, the particles that matter is made up of are to small to be seen. For example, think about what happens when you blow up a balloon. As you're blowing up the balloon you'll notice that it will start inflating. You won't see anything inside. This proves that the particles in matter are so small you won't be able to see them even though they still exist. The reason this doesn't work on liquids or solids in because in those particular states of the matter the particles that make up their matter are packed so closely together you see a big group of them.

When water is in it's solid state (ice) the water molecules are tightly packed together and not moving much. When water is in its liquid state (water) the molecules are slightly spread apart and moving around at a decent speed. When water is in its gas state (Water Vapor) the molecules are very far apart and moving at fast speeds. There is one more state of matter called plasma but we won't get into that.

Conservation Of Matter

Antoine Laurent Lavoisier created the scientific law that states, "The amount of matter stays the same even when it changes form." This law is called the conservation of matter. For example, lets mix 25 grams of sugar with 150 grams of water in a pre-weighed water glass. The sugar appears to have disappeared. However, when we weigh the glass of sugar and water it weighs 175 grams (After you subtract the glasses weight from it). Thus, the amount of matter stays the same even when it changes form.

Diffusion

Let's talk about diffusion. Diffusion is the movement of particles from a higher concentration to a lower concentration. In simpler terms diffusion is something going from a bigger to smaller amount. When their is a candle the smoke goes from a higher concentration of particles (above the particles) to a lower concentration of particles (10 inches away from the candle). This is because molecules don't like being close together. The smoke molecules separate from their main source around the candle. What affects the rate of a chemical reaction?

The rate of a chemical reaction is the speed at which a chemical reaction takes place. Things that affect the rate of a chemical reaction are the temperature, concentration, and pressure. The temperature can either slow the rate of a chemical reaction or increase it. The hotter the temperature, the more the molecules move around , and the faster the reaction takes place. The colder the temperature, the slower the molecules move around, and the reaction takes more time. The concentration of molecules affects a reaction because the closer the molecules the move likely they are to collide and create a reaction. The pressure affects a reaction because the higher the pressure the closer the molecules are. This is because all the molecules get compressed together from the pressure. Thus, the higher the pressure the faster the reaction.
